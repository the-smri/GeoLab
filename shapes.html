All About Shapes

Explore 2D and 3D shapes with detailed information

2D Shapes (Flat Shapes)

2D shapes are flat and have only two dimensions: length and width.

Circle

Properties:
  • Sides: 0 (curved line)
  • Angles: 0
  • Symmetry: Infinite lines of symmetry
  • Perimeter: Circumference = 2πr
  • Area: πr²
Real-Life Examples:
  • Coins
  • Wheels
  • Plates
  • Clock faces

Fun Fact: The circle is the shape with the largest area for a given perimeter.

Square

Properties:
  • Sides: 4 (all equal)
  • Angles: 4 right angles (90° each)
  • Symmetry: 4 lines of symmetry
  • Perimeter: 4 × side length
  • Area: side length × side length
Real-Life Examples:
  • Chessboard squares
  • Post-it notes
  • Window panes
  • Tile floors

Fun Fact: A square is a special type of rectangle where all sides are equal.

Rectangle

Properties:
  • Sides: 4 (opposite sides equal)
  • Angles: 4 right angles (90° each)
  • Symmetry: 2 lines of symmetry
  • Perimeter: 2 × (length + width)
  • Area: length × width
Real-Life Examples:
  • Doors
  • Books
  • Smartphones
  • Tables

Fun Fact: All squares are rectangles, but not all rectangles are squares.

Triangle

Properties:
  • Sides: 3
  • Angles: 3 (sum always 180°)
  • Types: Equilateral, Isosceles, Scalene
  • Perimeter: sum of all sides
  • Area: ½ × base × height
Real-Life Examples:
  • Pyramid sides
  • Sandwich halves
  • Roof structures
  • Tortilla chips

Fun Fact: Triangles are the strongest shape, which is why they're used in bridges and roofs.

Pentagon

Properties:
  • Sides: 5
  • Angles: 5 (sum is 540°)
  • Interior Angle: 108° (regular pentagon)
  • Symmetry: 5 lines of symmetry
Real-Life Examples:
  • Home plate in baseball
  • Some traffic signs
  • The Pentagon building
  • Flowers like morning glories

Fun Fact: "Penta" means five in Greek, so pentagon means "five angles".

Hexagon

Properties:
  • Sides: 6
  • Angles: 6 (sum is 720°)
  • Interior Angle: 120° (regular hexagon)
  • Symmetry: 6 lines of symmetry
Real-Life Examples:
  • Honeycomb cells
  • Snowflakes
  • Nut bolts
  • Some soccer ball patterns

Fun Fact: Hexagons tessellate perfectly (fit together without gaps), which is why bees use them in honeycombs.

3D Shapes (Solid Shapes)

3D shapes have three dimensions: length, width, and height.

Cube

Properties:
  • Faces: 6 (all squares)
  • Edges: 12
  • Vertices: 8 (corners)
  • Volume: side × side × side
  • Surface Area: 6 × side²
Real-Life Examples:
  • Dice
  • Ice cubes
  • Rubik's cube
  • Sugar cubes

Fun Fact: A cube is also called a "regular hexahedron" because it has six faces.

Sphere

Properties:
  • Faces: 1 curved surface
  • Edges: 0
  • Vertices: 0
  • Volume: (4/3)πr³
  • Surface Area: 4πr²
Real-Life Examples:
  • Balls (basketball, soccer ball)
  • Planets
  • Marbles
  • Oranges

Fun Fact: Of all shapes with the same volume, a sphere has the smallest surface area.

Cone

Properties:
  • Faces: 2 (1 flat circle, 1 curved surface)
  • Edges: 1 circular edge
  • Vertices: 1 (apex)
  • Volume: (1/3)πr²h
Real-Life Examples:
  • Ice cream cones
  • Traffic cones
  • Party hats
  • Volcanoes

Fun Fact: The cone shape helps things roll in a circle instead of a straight line.

Cylinder

Properties:
  • Faces: 3 (2 circles, 1 curved surface)
  • Edges: 2 circular edges
  • Vertices: 0
  • Volume: πr²h
Real-Life Examples:
  • Cans (soda cans, soup cans)
  • Pipes
  • Pillars
  • Batteries

Fun Fact: A cylinder can roll like a circle, but it only rolls in one direction.
